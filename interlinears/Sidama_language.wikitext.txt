'''Sidama''' or '''Sidaamu Afoo''' is an [[Afro-Asiatic languages|Afro-Asiatic language]] belonging to the [[Highland East Cushitic languages|Highland East Cushitic]] branch of the [[Cushitic languages|Cushitic]] family. It is spoken in parts of southern [[Ethiopia]] by the [[Sidama people]], particularly in the densely populated [[Sidama Region|Sidama National Regional State]] (SNRS). Sidaamu Afoo is the ethnic [[Exonym and endonym|autonym]] for the language, while Sidaminya is its name in [[Amharic language|Amharic]]. Although it is not known to have any specific dialects, it shares over 64% lexical similarity with [[Alaba-K’abeena language|Alaba-K'abeena]], 62% with [[Kambaata language|Kambaata]], and 53% with [[Hadiyya language|Hadiyya]], all of which are other languages spoken in southwestern [[Ethiopia]]. The word order is typically SOV. Sidaama has over 100,000 [[Second language|L2]] speakers. The literacy rate for [[First language|L1]] speakers is 1%-5%, while for L2 speakers it is 20%. In terms of its writing, Sidaama used an Ethiopic script up until 1993, from which point forward it has used a Latin script.<ref>Raymond G. Gordon Jr., ed.  2005.  ''Ethnologue:  Languages of the World''.  15th edition.  Dallas:  Summer Institute of Linguistics.</ref>

The term ''Sidamo'' has also been used by some authors to refer to larger groupings of East Cushitic and even [[Omotic languages]].<ref>Ring, Trudy, Noelle Watson, and Paul Schellinger. [https://books.google.com/books?id=R44VRnNCzAYC&printsec=frontcover&source=gbs_ge_summary_r&cad=0#v=onepage&q&f=false "International Dictionary of Historic Places: Middle East and Africa, Volume 4."] 1994.</ref> The languages within this Sidamo grouping contain similar, alternating phonological features.<ref>Leslau, Wolf. [https://www.jstor.org/stable/596302 "Sidamo Features in the South Ethiopic Phonology."] "Journal of the American Oriental Society," 1959.</ref> The results from a research study conducted in 1968-1969 concerning mutual intelligibility between different Sidamo languages suggest that Sidaama is more closely related to the [[Gedeo language]], which it shares a border with to the south, than other Sidamo languages.<ref>Bender, Marvin L. and Robert L. Cooper. [http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/002438417190074X "Mutual Intelligibility Within Sidamo."] 1979.</ref> According to the ''Ethnologue'', the two languages share a lexical similarity of 60%.<ref>Raymond G. Gordon Jr., ed.  2005.  ''Ethnologue:  Languages of the World''.  15th edition.  Dallas:  Summer Institute of Linguistics.</ref> Sidaama vocabulary has been influenced by [[Oromo language|Oromo]] vocabulary.

== Grammar ==

=== Noun Phrases ===
In Sidaama, not all [[noun phrase]]s have [[noun]]s. This can occur when it is so obvious what kind of thing the [[referent]] of the noun phrase is, that it is unnecessary for the speaker to mention it. Sidaama has two types of noun phrases without nouns. One type is made up only of an [[adjective]] or a [[Numeral (linguistics)|numeral]], where the adjective or the numeral agrees in case, number, and gender with the referent of a noun phrase. This is shown in the examples below:

{{interlinear|indent=2
|busul-u da-ø-ino.
|smart-NOM.M come-3SG.M-PERF.3
|‘The smart one (masculine) came.’}}

{{interlinear|indent=2
|sas-u da-ø-ino
|three-NOM.M come-3SG.M-PERF.3
|‘The three (masculine) came.’}}

The other type of noun phrase without a noun is formed with a noun-phrase [[clitic]], or <small>NPC</small>. This <small>NPC</small> starts with ''t'' (<small>FEM</small>) or ''h'' (<small>MASC</small>). This is thought to originate from the Afro-Asiatic [[demonstrative]] containing ''t'' (<small>FEM</small>) or ''k'' (<small>MASC</small>). The Sidaama <small>NPC</small> appears in various forms. Which form is used then depends on the gender of the referent of the noun phrase, and the syntactic role or case of the noun phrase. When a noun phrase without a noun is formed with an <small>NPC</small>, both the speaker and the listener know its referent. In this case, the <small>NPC</small> attaches to the end of a [[Genitive case|genitive]] noun phrase or relative clause to form a noun phrase without a noun. This is shown in the examples below:

{{interlinear|indent=2
|isí{{=}}ti ba’-’-ino.
|3SG.M.GEN{{=}}NPC.F.NOM disappear-3SG.F-PERF.3
|‘His (FEM) disappeared.’}}

{{interlinear|indent=2
|ani ku’uí beett-í{{=}}ta seekk-o-mm-o.
|1SG.NOM that.M.GEN child-GEN.M.MOD{{=}}NPC.F.ACC repair-PERF.1-1SG-M
|‘I (MASC) repaired that boy’s (FEM).’<ref>Kawachi, Kazuhiro. [https://researchmap.jp/?action=cv_download_main&upload_id=156457 "Noun Phrases Without Nouns in Sidaama (Sidamo)."] 2011.</ref>}}

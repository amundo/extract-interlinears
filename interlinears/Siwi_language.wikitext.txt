'''Siwi''' (also known as '''Siwan'''<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Stanley|first1=C. V. B.|title=The Siwan Language and Vocabulary, Proper Names, Siwan Money, Weights and Measures (Continued from the Journal of April, 1912.)|journal=Journal of the Royal African Society|date=1912|volume=11|issue=44|pages=438–457}}</ref> or '''Siwa Berber''';<ref>{{cite book|last1=Kossmann|first1=Maarten|title=The Arabic Influence on Northern Berber|date=2013|publisher=Brill|location=Leiden|isbn=978-90-04-25308-7|page=25}}</ref> native name: '''''Jlan n isiwan''''') is the easternmost [[Berber language]], spoken in [[Egypt]] by an estimated 15,000<ref name="souag-book">{{cite book|last1=Souag|first1=Lameen|title=Berber and Arabic in Siwa (Egypt)|date=2013|publisher=Rüdiger Köppe|location=Köln|isbn=978-3-89645-937-4|page=15}}</ref><ref name="souag">''Grammatical Contact in the Sahara: Arabic, Berber, and Songhay in Tabelbala and Siwa'', Lameen Souag, PhD thesis, SOAS, 2010</ref> to 20,000<ref name="e18" /> people in the [[oasis|oases]] of [[Siwa Oasis|Siwa]] and [[Qara Oasis|Gara]], near the [[Egypt–Libya border|Libyan border]].

Siwi is the normal language of daily communication among the Egyptian Berbers of Siwa and Gara, but because it is not taught at local schools, used in the media nor recognised by the Egyptian government, its long-term survival may be threatened by contacts with outsiders and by the use of [[Egyptian Arabic]] in mixed marriages;<ref>{{cite book|last1=Schiattarella|first1=Valentina|title=Berber Texts from Siwa (Egypt) - including a Grammatical Sketch|date=2016|publisher=Rüdiger Köppe|location=Köln|isbn=978-3-89645-946-6|page=19}}</ref> nearly all Siwis today learn to speak Egyptian Arabic as a second language from an early age.<ref>(Souag 2013:17)</ref>

Siwi has been heavily influenced by [[Arabic language|Arabic]], notably Egyptian and [[Bedouin Arabic|Bedouin]],<ref>(Souag 2013:32-33)</ref> but also earlier stages of Arabic.<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Souag|first1=Lameen|title=Siwa and its significance for Arabic dialectology|journal=Zeitschrift für Arabische Linguistik|date=2009|volume=51|pages=51–75}}</ref>

Siwi is the only Berber language indigenous to Egypt and is natively spoken further east than any other Berber variety of North Africa. Within Berber, it stands out for a number of unusual linguistic features, including the collapse of gender distinctions in the plural, the absence of dedicated negative forms of the verb, the use of full finite agreement on the verb in subject relativisation, the use of ''la'' for sentential negation and the borrowing from Arabic of a productive comparative form for adjectives.  Siwi also shows a typological feature that is strikingly rare, not only regionally but also worldwide: addressee agreement on demonstratives.

==Classification==
Siwi was traditionally associated with the [[Zenati languages|Zenati]] subgroup of Berber, following the 15th century historian al-Maqrizi,<ref>{{cite book|last1=المقريزي|first1=أحمد بن علي بن عبد القادر|title=المواعظ والاعتبار بذكر الخطط والآثار|date=1997|publisher=دار الكتب العلمية|location=بيروت|page=435|url=http://islamport.com/k/bld/5025/433.htm}}</ref> and Destaing (1920/3)<ref>Edmond Destaing, "Note sur la conjugaison des verbes de forme C1eC2", ''Mémoires de la Société Linguistique de Paris'', 22 (1920/3), pp. 139-148</ref> treated it as part of a "groupe du Nord" including Zenati, on the basis of similarities in the verbal system. Vycichl (2005)<ref>Werner Vycichl.  2005.  "Jlân n Isîwan:  Sketch of the Berber Language of the Oasis of Siwa (Egypt)," ''Berberstudien & A Sketch of Siwi Berber (Egypt).''  Ed. Dymitr Ibriszimow & Maarten Kossmann.  Berber Studies, vol. 10.  Cologne: Rüdiger Köppe Verlag. p. 188{{ISBN|3-89645-389-0}}</ref> notes that it shares the feature of prefix vowel reduction with Zenati. Aikhenvald and Militarev (1984),<ref>{{cite book|last1=Aikhenvald|first1=Alexandra Y.|last2=Militarev|first2=A. Ju.|title=IV vsesojuznaja konferencija afrikanistov 'Afrika v 80-e gody: itogi i perspektivy razvitija' (Moskva, 3-5 oktjabrja 1984 g.), II|date=1984|publisher=Institut Afrika Akademii Nauk SSSR|location=Moscow|pages=83–85|chapter=Klassifikacja livijsko-guančskih jazykov}}</ref> followed by ''[[Ethnologue]]''<ref name="eth">{{cite web|url=http://www.ethnologue.com/show_language.asp?code=siz|title=Siwi|work=Ethnologue}}</ref> placed Siwi in an [[Eastern Berber languages|Eastern Berber]] group, along with [[Awjila language|Awjila]] and [[Sokna language|Sokna]] in eastern and central [[Libya]]. Kossmann (1999)<ref>Kossmann, Maarten. 1999. Essai sur la phonologie du proto-berbère. Köln: Köppe.</ref> links it with Sokna and the [[Nafusi language|Nafusi]] dialect cluster of western Libya and Tunisia, but not with Awjila. Souag (2013)<ref>(Souag 2013:17-26)</ref> similarly argues, based on shared innovations, that Siwi emerged from a dialect continuum stretching between Nafusi and Sokna that excluded Awjila, and went on to have some influence on Awjila after this dialect continuum's breakup.

The "[[Endangered Languages Project]]"<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.endangeredlanguages.com/lang/295|title=Endangered Languages Project - Siwi|work=The Endangered Languages}}</ref> classifies the Siwa language as vulnerable to extinction, listing a 20% certainty based on compiled evidence.<ref>Brenzinger Matthias. 2007. "Language Endangerment in Northern Africa." Matthias Brenzinger- Mouton de Gruyter. Ch.6: 123-139</ref><ref>Moseley, Christopher. 2010. "Atlas of the World’s Languages in Danger." Christopher Moseley (edt.) 3rd edn.</ref>

The transcription of these consonants differs somewhat from source to source.  Naumann<ref>(Naumann 2012:312)</ref> proposes a practical Latin-based transcription inspired by common practice in other Berber languages: pharyngealised consonants are transcribed with an underdot (e.g., ṭ for {{IPA|/tˤ/}}), postalveolars are written with a hacek (č, ğ, š for {{IPA|/tʃ/}}, {{IPA|/dʒ/}}, {{IPA|/ʃ/}}), semivowel {{IPA|/j/}} as y, uvular fricatives as corresponding velars (x, ɣ) and epiglottals as ḥ, ɛ.  However, the epiglottals are often instead transcribed as corresponding pharyngeals ħ, ʕ, avoiding the danger of mistaking <ɛ> for a vowel, while the voiced postalveolar affricate/fricative is often written as j or ž.  All sources transcribe the glottal fricative as h.

===Vowels===
Siwi has six phonemic vowels: {{IPA|/a, e, i, o, u, ə/}}.<ref>(Naumann 2012:273)</ref> The mid vowels {{IPA|/e/}} and {{IPA|/o/}} are excluded from word-final position, and {{IPA|/o/}} is rare. The presence of mid vowels is unusual for a Berber language, and largely reflects Siwi-specific sound changes as well as borrowing from dialectal Arabic; before these changes, the proto-Berber distinction between *i and *e had been neutralized in every environment except before word-final {{IPA|/n/}}.<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Souag|first1=Lameen|last2=Van Putten|first2=Marijn|title=The origin of mid vowels in Siwi|journal=Studies in African Linguistics|date=2016|volume=45|issue=1 & 2|pages=189–208|url=http://sal.research.pdx.edu/PDF/450Souag.pdf}}</ref>

==Grammar==
The basic [[word order]] of Siwi is subject-verb-object,<ref>{{cite book|last1=Schiattarella|first1=Valentina|title=Berber Texts from Siwa (Egypt) - including a Grammatical Sketch|date=2016|publisher=Rüdiger Köppe|location=Köln|isbn=978-3-89645-946-6|page=60}}</ref> as in:

{{interlinear|indent=3|lang=siz
|akúḅḅi la yušas náčču i támẓa
|boy not he.gave.her food to ogress
|'The boy didn't give food to the ogress.'<ref>{{cite book|last1=Souag|first1=Lameen|title=Berber and Arabic in Siwa (Egypt)|date=2013|publisher=Rüdiger Köppe|location=Köln|isbn=978-3-89645-937-4|page=216}}</ref>
}}

Prepositions precede the noun phrase. Within the noun phrase, numerals (except, sometimes, "one") precede the noun quantified,<ref>(Souag 2013:131-133)</ref> while other modifiers follow the head noun. Demonstratives always follow adjectives or possessive suffixes, and may even follow relative clauses,<ref>(Souag 2013:146-147)</ref> e.g.:

{{interlinear|indent=3|lang=siz
|akbər ə́nnəw aməllal dawok
|robe my white that
|'that white robe of mine'}}

===Nouns===
Siwi nouns are specified for [[Grammatical gender|gender]] (masculine or feminine) and [[Grammatical number|number]] (singular or plural; on the occasional occurrence of duals, see [[#Numerical system|Numerical system]] below). Most nouns incorporate a fixed prefix, usually ''a-'' for masculine singular (e.g., {{transl|siz|asen}} "tooth"), ''i-'' for masculine plural (e.g., {{transl|siz|isenən}} "teeth"), ''ta-'' for feminine singular (e.g., {{transl|siz|taṣṛəṃt}} "intestine"), ''ti-'' for feminine plural (e.g., {{transl|siz|tiṣəṛṃen}} "intestines").<ref>(Souag 2013:62)</ref> Arabic loans often start with invariant ''(ə)l-'', usually assimilating to a following coronal, e.g., {{transl|siz|ləqləm}} "pen", {{transl|siz|ddhan}} "oil".<ref>(Souag 2013:78)</ref> Many nouns also incorporate a suffix, usually feminine singular ''-t'', masculine plural ''-ən'', feminine plural ''-en'', as seen above; Arabic loans often show a feminine singular suffix ''-ət'' or ''-a'', and a feminine plural suffix ''-at'' or {{transl|siz|-iyyat}}, e.g., {{transl|siz|ɣṛaḅa}} "raven" vs. {{transl|siz|ɣṛaḅiyyat}} "ravens".<ref>(Souag 2013:74)</ref> Pluralization is often also marked on the stem itself by internal changes, e.g., {{transl|siz|azidi}} "jackal" vs. {{transl|siz|izida}} "jackals", {{transl|siz|ašṭiṭ}} "bird" vs. {{transl|siz|išəṭṭan}} "birds".<ref>(Souag 2013:62-63)</ref>

In a noun either the last syllable or the second-to-last (penultimate) is [[Stress (linguistics)|stressed]], depending on context. The factors determining stress in the noun remain a matter of debate. According to Souag (2013),<ref>(Souag 2013:80-82)</ref> stress depends essentially on definiteness: definite nouns receive penultimate stress, while indefinites are stressed on the last syllable. Schiattarella (2017)<ref>{{cite web|last1=Schiattarella|first1=Valentina|title=The accent functions on nouns in Siwi|url=https://www.academia.edu/33549797|website=Academia.edu}}</ref> argues that the situation is somewhat more complicated: notably, locatives and right detached nouns receive accent on the last syllable, while left detached nouns are stressed on the penultimate.

Unlike most larger Berber languages, Siwi has no [[Construct state#Berber|state]] distinction: a noun takes the same form whether used as subject or as object.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Laoust|first1=Emile|title=Siwa I : son parler|date=1932|publisher=Ernest Leroux|location=Paris|page=97}}</ref>

However, agreement is not always complete. Feminine plural nouns often show masculine plural agreement.<ref>(Souag 2013:92)</ref><ref>(Schiattarella 2016:42)</ref>

Adjectives may be marked with a suffix ''-a'',<ref>Werner Vycichl.  2005.  "Jlân n Isîwan:  Sketch of the Berber Language of the Oasis of Siwa (Egypt)," ''Berberstudien & A Sketch of Siwi Berber (Egypt).''  Ed. Dymitr Ibriszimow & Maarten Kossmann. Gradable adjectives with no more than three root consonants form an invariant comparative based on the consonantal template ''(ə)CCəC'', originally borrowed from Arabic: thus ''aħəkkik'' "small" yields ''əħkək'' "smaller", ''agzal'' "short" yields ''gzəl'' "smaller", ''aẓəy'' "bitter" yields ''ẓya'' "more bitter".<ref>{{cite book|last1=Walker|first1=W. Seymour|title=The Siwi Language|date=1921|publisher=Kegan Paul|location=London|page=32}}</ref><ref>Werner Vycichl.  2005.  "Jlân n Isîwan:  Sketch of the Berber Language of the Oasis of Siwa (Egypt)," ''Berberstudien & A Sketch of Siwi Berber (Egypt).''  Ed. Dymitr Ibriszimow & Maarten Kossmann.  Berber Studies, vol. 10.  Cologne: Rüdiger Köppe Verlag. p. 212{{ISBN|3-89645-389-0}}</ref><ref>(Souag 2013:102-104)</ref> Adding a suffix ''-hŭm'' to this in turn yields the superlative.

===Demonstratives===
Demonstratives agree with their referent in number and, if singular, in gender; medial demonstratives also agree with the addressee, a typologically unusual type of [[allocutive agreement]].<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Souag|first1=Lameen|title=The development of addressee agreement on demonstratives|journal=Diachronica|date=2014|volume=31|issue=4|pages=535–563|doi=10.1075/dia.31.4.04sou}}</ref> The pronominal demonstratives are as follows:
* "this" (proximal): m. ''wa / waya'', f. ''ta / taya'', pl. {{transl|siz|wi / wiyya}}
* "this/that" (medial, speaking to a man): m. ''wok'', f. ''tok'', pl. {{transl|siz|wiyyok}}
* "this/that" (medial, speaking to a woman): m. ''wom'', f. ''tom'', pl. {{transl|siz|wiyyom}}
* "this/that" (medial, speaking to a group): m. ''werwən'', f. ''terwən'', pl. {{transl|siz|wiyyerwən}}
* "that" (distal): m. ''wih'', f. ''tih'', pl. ''widin''

When a demonstrative modifies a noun phrase, it takes a prefix ''da-'' (''ta-'' for feminine singular). To form a [[Presentative (grammar)|presentative]] ("here is..."), it instead takes a prefix ''ɣ-''. Placeholders ("whatsit", "whatchamacallit") use the singular distal forms plus ''-in'' ({{transl|siz|wihin}}, {{transl|siz|tihin}}).

Demonstrative adverbials are based on the same series minus referent agreement markers: proximal ''-a / -aya'', medial ''-ok / -om / -erwən'', distal ''-ih''. Locative adverbs ("here", "there") prefix to these ''gd-'' (or approximate locative ''ss-''), while adverbs of manner ("like this", "like that") prefix ''ams-''.

===Verbs===
Siwi verbs agree in person, number, and (when singular) gender with their subjects and their indirect objects,<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Souag|first1=Lameen|title=The Development of Dative Agreement in Berber: Beyond Nominal Hierarchies|journal=Transactions of the Philological Society|date=2015|volume=113|issue=2|pages=213–248|doi=10.1111/1467-968X.12049|url=https://halshs.archives-ouvertes.fr/halshs-01376203/file/postprint-souag2015_development_of_dative_agreement_in_berber.pdf}}</ref> and take suffixes marking pronominal objects. The verb "open", for example, is conjugated in the perfective as follows:<ref>Werner Vycichl.  2005.  "Jlân n Isîwan:  Sketch of the Berber Language of the Oasis of Siwa (Egypt)," ''Berberstudien & A Sketch of Siwi Berber (Egypt).''  Ed. Dymitr Ibriszimow & Maarten Kossmann.  Berber Studies, vol. 10.  Cologne: Rüdiger Köppe Verlag. p. 226{{ISBN|3-89645-389-0}}</ref>

: ''fətk-ax'' I opened
: ''fətk-aṭ'' you (sg.) opened
: ''yə-ftək'' he opened
: ''tə-ftək'' she opened
: ''nə-ftək'' we opened
: ''fətk-əm'' you (pl.) opened
: ''yə-ftk-ən'' they opened

In some cases, plural nouns trigger feminine singular agreement.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Schiattarella|first1=Valentina|title=Berber Texts from Siwa (Egypt) - including a Grammatical Sketch|date=2016|publisher=Rüdiger Köppe|location=Köln|isbn=978-3-89645-946-6|page=52}}</ref>

The order of pronominal affixes on the verb is as follows: (subject)-stem-(subject)-(indirect object)-(direct object), e.g., {{transl|siz|y-uš-as-t i talti}} "he gave it (m.) to the woman".<ref>(Schiattarella 2016:31)</ref>

Siwi verbs are also marked for [[grammatical aspect|aspect]] and [[grammatical mood|mood]]. The basic stem is used in the imperative and in the irrealis/aorist; the latter normally takes a prefix ''ga-'' (preceding agreement suffixes), or {{transl|siz|(əd)da-}} for suggestives. The perfective form is identical to the stem for most verbs, but in a few is marked by a variable suffixed vowel. The imperfective is formed from the stem by a variety of morphological strategies, including gemination of the second consonant, ''t'' prefixation, and insertion of an ''a''. A special perfect/resultative (unusual within Berber) is formed from the perfective by suffixing ''-a'' to a fully conjugated perfective verb including any suffixes, changing ''ə'' in the last syllable to ''i''; the same procedure, applied to an imperfective verb, yields the meaning "while".<ref>(Souag 2013:182-199)</ref> Thus, for example, from the verb ''ukəl'' "walk" Siwi derives:<ref>(Schiattarella 2016:46)</ref>

* perfective ''y-ukəl'' "he walked"
* resultative ''y-ukil-a'' "he has walked"
* imperfective ''i-takəl'' "he walks, he is walking, he was walking"
* imperfective+a ''i-takil-a'' "while he is/was walking"
* ga+aorist ''g-(y)-ukəl'' "he will walk, he would walk"
* ədda+aorist ''ədd-(y)-ukəl'' "let him walk!"

Unlike many Berber languages, Siwi has no special verbal morphology for negation; in all aspects and moods, verbs are simply negated with the preverbal particle ''la''. The prohibitive ("do not"), however, uses the imperfective form of the verb, unlike the imperative which uses the basic stem.

==Numerical system==
The Siwi numerical system is almost entirely borrowed from Arabic; speakers have only retained two traditional Berber numerals, one and two, which are used rather consistently for qualifying nouns but compete with Arabic equivalents for the purpose of counting.<ref name="Christfried Naumann 2009">{{cite web|last1=Naumann|first1=Christfried|title=Siwa (Siwi)|url=https://mpi-lingweb.shh.mpg.de/numeral/Siwa.htm|website=Numeral Systems of the World's Languages|publisher=Max-Planck-Institut für Menschheitsgeschichte|date=2009}}</ref> The numerals 3-10 have the same form whether used for counting or for qualifying nouns.<ref name="Christfried Naumann 2009"/> Numbers 11-19 are described by Naumann (2009) as having two separate forms for counting and qualifying nouns.<ref name="Christfried Naumann 2009"/> The table that follows is from Naumann (2009),<ref name="Christfried Naumann 2009"/> and (following the source) uses IPA rather than practical transcription.

Some speakers preserve a feminine form for inherited "two", ''ssnət''<ref>{{cite book|last1=Schiattarella|first1=Valentina|title=Berber Texts from Siwa (Egypt) - including a Grammatical Sketch|date=2016|publisher=Rüdiger Köppe|location=Köln|isbn=978-3-89645-946-6|page=58}}</ref>

A further complication in the numeral system is the systematic use of duals and special bound forms of numerals with units of measurement borrowed from Arabic; thus from ''ssənt'' "year" we get ''sənt-en'' "two years" rather than using ''sən'' or ''tnen'', and from ''ssbuʕ'' "week" we get ''təlt sbuʕ-at'' (with ''təlt'' rather than ''tlata'' for "three").<ref>{{cite book|last1=Souag|first1=Lameen|title=Berber and Arabic in Siwa (Egypt)|date=2013|publisher=Rüdiger Köppe|location=Köln|isbn=978-3-89645-937-4|pages=113–117}}</ref>

==Literature==
Siwi is not a written language, in the sense that Siwi people normally write in Standard Arabic. It is, however, the vehicle of a little-documented [[Oral tradition|oral literature]]. Among the relatively few materials published, four genres are conspicuous: song lyrics or [[poetry|poems]], [[fairy tale]]s, [[riddle]]s, and [[proverb]]s.

===Verse===
Siwi verse is written in [[rhyme]], and is usually associated with song.  Sung poetry, or ''adyaz'', is performed mainly in bachelors' gatherings and tends to relate to love, whereas religious poetry (''ləqṣidət'') is recited.<ref>(Souag 2013:15-16, 276)</ref>  Malim (2001:90-92, 96) distinguishes songs, led by one man, from poems, shorter verse works recited antiphonally by groups at weddings; both are accompanied by the music of drums and horns.  In previous centuries these songs appear to have been of great symbolic importance to Siwi young men: a civil war in the oasis in 1712 was apparently terminated by a treaty including the stipulation that:

:: "if one of the Western ''zaggālah'' [bachelor farm workers] was singing in a garden, while doing his work there, and stopped, then one of the ''zaggālah'' of the Easterners should begin to sing and finish his song; the Westerner was not allowed to sing once more."<ref>{{cite book|last1=Fakhry|first1=Ahmed|title=Siwa Oasis|date=1973|publisher=American University in Cairo Press|location=Cairo|isbn=977-424-123-1|page=33}}</ref>

The earliest Siwi lyrics to be published are those gathered by Bricchetti-Robetti (1889);<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Bricchetti-Robetti|first1=Luigi|title=Sul dialetto di Siuwah|journal=Atti della Reale Accademia dei Lincei|date=1889|volume=286|issue=5|url=http://emeroteca.braidense.it/beic_attacc/sfoglia_articolo.php?IDTestata=927&CodScheda=00AI&IDT=34&IDV=387&IDF=0&IDA=14502}}</ref> others have been published in Jawharī (1949)<ref>{{cite book|last1=الجوهري|first1=رفعة|title=جنة الصحراء : سيوة وواحة أمون|date=1949|publisher=دار القومية|location=القاهرة|pages=209–219}}</ref> and Souag (2013),<ref>(Souag 2013:274-278)</ref> while Abd Allah (1917:26-27)<ref>{{cite book|last1=Abd Allah|first1=Mahmud Mohammad|chapter=Siwan Customs|editor1-last=Bates|editor1-first=Oric|title=Varia Africana I|date=1917|publisher=African Department, Peabody Museum, Harvard University|location=Harvard, MA|pages=2–28}}</ref> and Malim (2001:92-97) provide several songs and poems in translation.  The songs were also studied from a musicological perspective by Schiffer (1936).<ref>{{cite book|last1=Schiffer|first1=Brigitte|title=Die Oase Siwa und ihre Musik|date=1936|publisher=Wilhelm Postberg|location=Bottrop i. Westfalen}}</ref>  The following extract from a love song<ref>(Souag 2013:275)</ref> may give an idea of the genre:

===Tales===
A Siwi tale (''tanf̣ast'') uses a specific opening formula:<ref>(Schiattarella 2016:21-22)</ref>

: ''tixəṛxaṛén, tibəṛbaṛén, tiqəṭṭušén, g álbab n alħošə́nnax''
: "''tixəṛxaṛen, tibəṛbaṛen'' at the door of our courtyard"

and closes with the formula:

: ''ħattuta, ħattuta, qəṣṣəṛ ʕṃəṛha. akəṃṃús n əlxér i ənšní, akəṃṃús n šáṛ i əntnə́n''
: "Tale, tale, it has shortened its span.  A bundle of goodness to us, a bundle of badness to them."

They were typically told by old women to children on evenings to entertain and perhaps to educate them.<ref>(Schiattarella 2016:25, 71-73)</ref>  Since the arrival of television in the oasis, this practice has largely disappeared.<ref>(Schiattarella 2016:25)</ref>  Apart from humans and (talking) animals, a common character in such tales is the [[ogre]] (''amẓa'') or ogress (''tamẓa'').  The first Siwi tales to appear in print were four short fables gathered from men ("The Jackal and the Ewe", "The Jackal and the Hyena", "The Hare, the Jackal, the Hyena, and the Lion", and "The Magic Ring") in Laoust (1932:146-150).  Malim (2001)<ref>{{cite book|last1=Malim|first1=Fathy|title=Oasis Siwa: From the Inside. Traditions, customs, magic|date=2001|publisher=Al Katan|location=Egypt|pages=100–104}}</ref> gives two Siwi folk tales ("The Green Cow" and "The King's Daughter and the Three Beautiful Girls") in English translation.  Schiattarella (2016) transcribes and translates fourteen tales, gathered from women.

===Riddles===
Malim (2001:85) describes riddles as "once the preferred pastime of Siwi women", who would meet at night to exchange them, but notes that they have largely been superseded by watching television.  Few Siwi riddles have been published; Malim (2001:85-87) gives some twenty, while Schiattarella (2016:117) records four, including:

{| class="wikitable"
|-
| ''itákəl g əlqášš,'' || It walks in the straw 
|-
| ''l-itə́ṃṃəl xášš'' || and it doesn't rustle.
|-
| Answer: '' tlá'' || The shadow

===Proverbs===
Among existing publications on Siwi, only Malim (2001:95-85, 87-90) discusses proverbs in any detail, drawing a distinction between "morals", timeworn advice in proverbial form, and proverbs proper.  Examples of the former include "Wear clothes that others prefer, but eat and drink what you prefer"; of the latter, "A man who sells a cow, and asks for more money for the insect on it" (in his transcription, {{transl|siz|Yzenz tfonst, eftash aflokrad ines}}), mocking excessive concern about small sums.

'''Sulka''' is a [[language isolate]] of [[New Britain]], [[Papua New Guinea]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal|last1=Dunn|first1=Michael|last2=Levinson|first2=Stephen C.|last3=Lindström|first3=Eva|last4=Reesink|first4=Ger|last5=Terrill|first5=Angela|date=2008|title=Structural Phylogeny in Historical Linguistics: Methodological Explorations Applied in Island Melanesia|url=https://muse.jhu.edu/article/256769|journal=Language|language=en|volume=84|issue=4|pages=710–759|doi=10.1353/lan.0.0069|s2cid=6356461|issn=1535-0665|hdl=11858/00-001M-0000-0013-1F55-5|hdl-access=free}}</ref>  In 1991, there were 2,500 speakers in eastern [[Pomio District]], [[East New Britain Province]].<ref name="Ethnologue22-PNG">{{cite web |url=https://www.ethnologue.com/country/PG/languages |title=Papua New Guinea languages |work=[[Ethnologue]]: Languages of the World |edition=22nd |editor1-last=Eberhard |editor1-first=David M. |editor2-last=Simons |editor2-first=Gary F. |editor3-last=Fennig |editor3-first=Charles D. |date=2019 |location=Dallas |publisher=[[SIL International]]}}</ref> Villages include Guma ({{coord|-5.275769|152.092315|type:city_region:PG|name=Guma Village}}) in [[East Pomio Rural LLG]].<ref name=":1">Reesink, Ger. 2005. Sulka of East New Britain: A Mixture of Oceanic and Papuan Traits. ''Oceanic Linguistics'' 44. 145-193.</ref> With such a low population of speakers, this language is considered to be endangered.  Sulka speakers had originally migrated to East New Britain from [[New Ireland (island)|New Ireland]].<ref name="Stebbins-Island">{{cite book |last1=Stebbins |first1=Tonya |last2=Evans |first2=Bethwyn |last3=Terrill |first3=Angela |editor1-last=Palmer |editor1-first=Bill |date=2018 |title=The Languages and Linguistics of the New Guinea Area: A Comprehensive Guide |chapter=The Papuan languages of Island Melanesia |series= The World of Linguistics |volume=4 |location=Berlin |publisher=De Gruyter Mouton |pages=775–894 |isbn=978-3-11-028642-7}}</ref>

== Classification ==
Sulka may be described as having ancient Papuan (non-Austronesian) roots, which additionally displays morphosyntactic constructions and some vocabulary items associated with the Oceanic branch of Austronesian (i.e. languages of the St. George linkage such as [[Mali language|Mali]]).<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Reesink|first=Ger P.|date=2005|title=Sulka of East New Britain: A Mixture of Oceanic and Papuan Traits|url=http://muse.jhu.edu/content/crossref/journals/oceanic_linguistics/v044/44.1reesink.pdf|journal=Oceanic Linguistics|language=en|volume=44|issue=1|pages=145–193|doi=10.1353/ol.2005.0026|s2cid=142693537|issn=1527-9421}}</ref> Alternatively, it has been proposed as possibly related to [[Kol language (Papua New Guinea)|Kol]] or [[Baining languages|Baining]] as part of the [[East Papuan languages|East Papuan]] proposal, but Palmer (2018) treats Sulka as a [[language isolate]].<ref name="Palmer-LLNGA">{{cite book |last=Palmer |first=Bill |editor1-last=Palmer |editor1-first=Bill |date=2018 |title=The Languages and Linguistics of the New Guinea Area: A Comprehensive Guide |chapter=Language families of the New Guinea Area |series= The World of Linguistics |volume=4 |location=Berlin |publisher=De Gruyter Mouton |pages=1–20 |isbn=978-3-11-028642-7}}</ref>

Sulka has some influence from the [[Mengen language]].<ref name="Stebbins-Island"/>

Over 3,000 to 3,500 years ago, the linguistic ancestors of Sulka speakers arrived in the Papua New Guinea area.<ref name=":0" />

== History ==
Although the history of the language is not well known, it may display a mixture of Oceanic and Papuan language traits.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Reesink|first=Ger P.|date=2005|title=Sulka of East New Britain: A Mixture of Oceanic and Papuan Traits|url=http://muse.jhu.edu/content/crossref/journals/oceanic_linguistics/v044/44.1reesink.pdf|journal=Oceanic Linguistics|language=en|volume=44|issue=1|pages=145–193|doi=10.1353/ol.2005.0026|s2cid=142693537|issn=1527-9421}}</ref> These are languages Sulka came into contact with, when the peoples speaking these other languages populated the area in neighboring villages, around 3,200 years ago.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Michael Dunn|last2=Stephen C. Levinson|last3=Eva Lindström|last4=Ger Reesink|last5=Angela Terrill|date=2008|title=Structural Phylogeny in Historical Linguistics: Methodological Explorations Applied in Island Melanesia|url=http://muse.jhu.edu/content/crossref/journals/language/v084/84.4.dunn.html|journal=Language|language=en|volume=84|issue=4|pages=710–759|doi=10.1353/lan.0.0069|s2cid=6356461|issn=1535-0665|hdl=11858/00-001M-0000-0013-1F55-5|hdl-access=free}}</ref>

== Geographic Distribution ==
Sulka is spoken along the coastal region of [[Wide Bay, Papua New Guinea|Wide Bay]],<ref name=":1" /> on the Southern coast of the [[Gazelle Peninsula]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.sil.org/resources/publications/entry/23670|title=Sulka Grammar Essentials|last=Tharpe|first=Douglas|date=1996|website=SIL International Publications|access-date=October 15, 2019}}</ref> on the eastern side of [[New Britain|New Britain Island]], [[Independent State of Papua New Guinea|Papua New Guinea]]. Some estimate speakers to number as high as between 3,000 and 3,500.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://catalogue.nla.gov.au/Record/1721149|title=1980 national population census: final figures: provincial summary|date=1983|publisher=National Statistical Office|editor-last=Papua New Guinea|location=Port Moresby, Papua New Guinea}}</ref><ref name=":1" /> Reesink (2005) reports on some Sulka speakers who have intermingled in neighboring villages with speakers of other languages such as Mali, southeast of [[Kokopo]].<ref name=":1" />

=== Vowels ===
For its vowels, Sulka has a contrast between three front vowels: high, mid, and low, [i], [e], and [ε], but there is no instance of the central high vowel [ɨ].<ref name=":1" /> However, when it comes to vocalic contrasts, it is not always clear. The mid front vowel may fluctuate somewhere between close-mid [e] and the more central-close vowel [ɪ], pronounced like English ''i'' in 'in'.<ref name=":1" /> The sounds [o] and [u] often fluctuate with each other as in the example of  '1<small>SG</small> verbal pronoun' [ku] and [ko]. This pattern of fluctuation seems to commonly occur for high front vowels. When looking at the length of vowels, long vowels are often confused with diphthongs.

==Lexicon==
A great majority of Sulka's lexicon is not Oceanic/Austronesian as stated by Schneider. However, there are a few words that are shared between both Papuan and Oceanic.

Examples from Geelvink (2005):

* pun 'base', as in a ho ka pun 'the tree its base', reflects POC {{as written|*puqun}}. Laufer (1955:42) gives Mengen pun ~ Gunantuna (= Tolai) vuna as evidence for the presence of Mengen speakers along the Wide Bay before Sulka speakers arrived from South New Ireland. But Sulka pun is not a recent Mengen loan. Rath (1986, ex. 324) gives bega pu-na for 'tree base-3SG.POSS'.
* nut 'island' ~ POC *nusa, with reflexes such as nui in NNG and nua in PT, nuta in Southeast Solomonic (Ross, Pawley, and Osmond 2003:42).
* kus 'rain' appears to reflect POC *qusan (Ross, Pawley, and Osmond 2003:141); with kue as reflex in Mengen (Poeng dialect).
* kopoi 'fog' ~ POC {{as written|*kapu(t)}}; *kopu (Ross, Pawley, and Osmond 2003:140).
* malo 'skirt made of bark from the breadfruit tree'. The Sulka form is identical to the one found in Mengen and Kove of the North New Guinea linkage, rather than to mal as it appears in languages of the St. George linkage. Of course, it may be a recent direct borrowing from Mengen.<ref name=":1" />

Basic verb phrases are similar to Oceanic languages.  For a typical Austronesian sentence structure, it follows the Subject Verb Object word order whereas Papuan follows a Subject Object Verb word order.  Free pronouns mainly act as verbal or prepositional object.  Instead of having the bilabial nasal found on the free pronouns, first and third person plural have an initial velar.<ref name=":1" />  Additionally, the basic verb phrase consists of a subject proclitic indicating both subject person/number and aspect/mood.   This is followed by one or more verbs, a (pro)nominal object where necessary, and optional oblique constituents.<ref name=":1" />

==Masculine/Feminine==
Most Papuan languages have masculine and feminine distinctions.  However, the Sulka language does not follow this rule.  As for the Austronesian languages, where they have inclusive and exclusive opposition in nonsingular first person, Sulka does not follow them either (Sulka of East New Britain: A Mixture of Oceanic and Papuan Traits, Reesink, 2005).  As stated by Reesink, "There is not even a third person differentiation between feminine and masculine genders".

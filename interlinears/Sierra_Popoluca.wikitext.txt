'''Sierra Popoluca''', also known as '''Soteapanec''', '''Soteapan Zoque''', or '''Highland Popoluca''', is a developing [[Mixe–Zoque languages|Mixe-Zoquean language]] of the [[Zoque languages|Zoquean]] branch.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.ethnologue.com/language/poi|title=Popoluca, Highland|website=Ethnologue|language=en|access-date=2019-04-19}}</ref> It has 35,050 speakers (INALI, 2009)<ref name=":40">INALI (2009). [http://www.endangeredlanguages.com/lang/6169/samples/7846 ''Catálogo de las Lenguas Indígenas Nacionales: Variantes Lingüísticas de México con sus autodenominaciones y referencias geoestadísticas.''] México.</ref> who live in the southern part of [[Veracruz]], [[Mexico]]. Sierra Popoluca has two sister languages, [[Texistepec language|Texistepec]] and [[Ayapa Zoque|Ayapanec]], both of which are severely endangered.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=7}}</ref>

The word ''[[popoluca]]'' means "gibberish” in [[Nahuatl]], and the name Sierra Popoluca comes from the language being labelled as such at the time of conquest. To avoid the derogatory connotations of ''popoluca'', some researchers have adopted the name Soteapanec for the language instead (named after the largest municipality it is spoken in). However, modern speakers do not seem to be concerned with the history of the word and simply see it as the name of their language.<ref name=":1">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=4}}</ref> Natively, speakers refer to the language as ''Nuntajɨyi'', which means "true word," and themselves as ''Nundajɨypappɨc''.{{Citation needed|date=May 2019}}

== Distribution ==
Sierra Popoluca is spoken in the following municipalities:<ref name=":41">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=3}}</ref>

* [[Soteapan]]
* [[Tatahuicapan]]
* [[Hueyapan de Ocampo]]

Other communities where it is spoken include Catemaco, Piedra Labrada, and Santa Rosa Cintepec. [[Nahuatl]] and [[Spanish language|Spanish]] are also spoken in nearby areas, and have influenced Sierra Popoluca through language contact.<ref name=":51">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=6-8}}</ref>

Sierra Popoluca has [[Phonotactics|phonotactic]] restrictions on both onset and coda clusters. For onsets, only the clusters /tr/, /kr/, and /kw/ are allowed. ==Morphology==
Sierra Popoluca is an [[Agglutinative language|agglutinating]], [[Polysynthetic language|polysynthetic]] language whose morpheme inventory is primarily inflectional and consists of roughly an equal number of clitics and suffixes, with no prefixes. The morphological processes reduplication and compounding are also observed in Sierra Popoluca.<ref name=":18">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=81}}</ref>

Sierra Popoluca has three major word classes: nouns, verbs, and adjectives.<ref name=":18" />

=== Suffixes ===
Sierra Popoluca has 28 suffixes, all of which can be categorized as either [[Derivational morpheme|derivational]], [[Inflectional morphemes|inflectional]], or [[Valency (linguistics)|valency]] adjusting. === Reduplication ===

=== Relative word order ===
In terms of relative word order, Sierra Popoluca exhibits some structural features common to VO (verb initial) languages and some common to OV (verb final) languages. A few examples of these structures are given below:<ref name=":30">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=435-436}}</ref>

* Possessor precedes possessum (common to OV languages)
{{Interlinear|diablo ʔi+jos|devil 3POSS+hole|‘devil's hole (hell)’|indent=3|abbreviations=3POSS:third person possessive|italics1=no}}

* Auxiliary verb precedes main verb (common to VO languages)
{{Interlinear|püüxiny moj-W Ø+wej-i|man begin-COMPL 3ABS+cry-DEP|‘The man began to cry.’|indent=3|abbreviations=3ABS:third person absolutive|italics1=no}}

* Complementizer precedes complement clause (common to VO languages)
{{Interlinear|ʔi+ʔix-W+tyi+ʔam ʔiga+ʔüch dya ʔan+joʔy-kaʔ-W|3ERG+see-COMPL+just+IAM COMP+1PRO NEG 1ERG:EXCL+be.angry-INSTR-COMPL|‘He saw that I wasn't angry.’|indent=3|abbreviations=IAM:iamative; 1ERG:first person ergative; 3ERG:third person ergative; COMP:complementizer; 1PRO:first person pronoun|italics1=no}}

=== Order of nominal modifiers ===
Nouns in Sierra Popoluca can be modified by determiners, adjectives, quantifiers, possessors, and relative clauses.<ref name=":31">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=147}}</ref> Whether a modifier precedes or follows the noun it is modifying depends on the modifier, as illustrated below:
* Demonstratives precede the nouns they modify<ref name=":32">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=151-153}}</ref>
{{Interlinear|ʔoy-taʔm-W yüʔp kootzük|go.and.return-{{gcl|1:2PL|speech act participant plural}}-COMPL this hill|‘We went to these hills.’|indent=3|italics1=no}}
* Adjectives precede the nouns they modify<ref name=":33">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=168-169}}</ref>
{{Interlinear|ʔi+na+miny-wü puro muja kuy|3ERG+ASS+come-COMPL pure wet tree|‘He brought pure wet wood.’|indent=3|abbreviations=3ERG:third person ergative|italics1=no}}
* Relative clauses may either precede or follow the nouns they modify<ref name=":34">{{Cite book|title=A Grammar of Sierra Popoluca|last=Boudreault|first=Lynda|publisher=Walter de Gruyter GmbH|year=2018|isbn=978-3-11-041167-6|location=Boston,Berlin|pages=184-186}}</ref>
{{Interlinear|jeʔm ʔi+na+ʔity-yaj-W Ø+müjtam+püʔk tük|that 3ERG+ASS+be-3PL-COMPL 3ABS+big+REL house|‘They have houses that are big.’|indent=3|abbreviations=3ERG:third person ergative; 3ABS:third person absolutive|italics1=no}}
{{Interlinear|süʔüp na+miny-ü tuum puktuuku Ø+yagatz+püʔk|now ASS+come-IMP one cloth 3ABS+long+REL|‘Now, bring a cloth that's long.’|indent=3|abbreviations=3ABS:third person absolutive|italics1=no}}

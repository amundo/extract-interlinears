'''Nottoway''' {{IPAc-en|ˈ|n|ɒ|t|ə|ˌ|w|eɪ}}, also called ''Cheroenhaka'', was a language spoken by the [[Nottoway people]]. Nottoway is closely related to [[Tuscarora language|Tuscarora]] within the [[Iroquoian languages|Iroquoian language family]]. Two tribes of Nottoway are recognized by the state of [[Virginia]]: the Nottoway Indian Tribe of Virginia and the Cheroenhaka (Nottoway) Indian Tribe. Other Nottoway descendants live in [[Wisconsin]] and [[Canada]], where some of their ancestors fled in the 18th century. The last known speaker, [[Edith Turner]], died in 1838. The Nottoway people are undertaking work for language revival.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ethnologue.com/show_language.asp?code=ntw|title=Nottoway|date=2009|publisher=Ethnologue|access-date=January 18, 2013}}</ref>

Knowledge of Nottoway comes primarily from a word list collected on March 4, 1820. Former President [[Thomas Jefferson]]’s handwritten letter to [[Peter Stephen Du Ponceau|Peter S. Du Ponceau]], on July 7, 1820, states that a Nottoway Indian vocabulary was obtained on March 4th, 1820 from Edith Turner, styled as their “Queen,” by [[John Wood (professor)|John Wood]], a former Professor of Mathematics at the [[College of William and Mary]].<ref>[http://www.cheroenhaka-nottoway.org/about-nottoway-tribe/nottoway-language.htm Thomas Jefferson to Peter S. Du Ponceau, July 7, 1820]</ref> Du Ponceau recognized the language immediately as Iroquoian, writing that he was "struck as well as astonished at its decided Iroquois Physiognomy."<ref name=":1">[http://www.encyclopediavirginia.org/Letter_from_Peter_S_DuPonceau_to_Thomas_Jefferson_1820 Peter S. DuPonceau to Thomas Jefferson, July 12, 1820], ''The Thomas Jefferson Papers'', Series 1, The Library of Congress.</ref> [[Blair A. Rudes]] (1981) concluded that Nottoway is a distinct language from Tuscarora, but closest to Tuscarora within Iroquoian.<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal|last=Rudes|first=Blair|date=1981|title=A Sketch of the Nottoway Language from a Historical-Comparative Perspective|journal=International Journal of American Linguistics|volume=47|pages=27–49|doi=10.1086/465672|s2cid=145679705}}</ref>

In addition to the vocabulary collected by John Wood, a few additional words were gathered by [[James Trezvant]].<ref>[http://www.encyclopediavirginia.org/Languages_and_Interpreters_in_Early_Virginia_Indian_Society#start_entry Encyclopedia Virginia]</ref>

=== Consonants ===
Nottoway has ten consonant phonemes, listed in the table below. Like the vowels, these consonant phonemes were reconstructed by Rudes using John Wood's vocabulary and knowledge of related languages.<ref name=":0" /> Most of the symbols that Rudes uses are the same as the IPA symbols. Where they differ, the IPA symbol is included in square brackets. The three labial consonants are in parentheses because these phonemes are only present in five words of the language, none of which are of Iroquoian descent. The letter ‘m’ also sometimes occurs at the end of a word after a vowel, but this is to indicate nasalization of the previous vowel, not the presence of the phoneme /m/.<ref name=":0"/>{{rp|29}}

=== Syllable structure ===
The English-based spelling Wood used makes it difficult to determine syllable structure. Most words, however, are consistent with the [[Syllable#Typical model|syllable structure]] (C)V(C)(C):

There is also limited evidence that words could end in three consonants:
{| class="wikitable"

|}
Consonant clusters must include /w/ or /s/, and possibly /n/. /w/ is the most common, but /s/ is still regularly seen in words like ''Whisk'' 'five'. The status of /n/ is uncertain since Wood used {{angbr|n}} to represent [[nasal vowel]]s.

Aside from ''Whisk'' 'five', most content words are multisyllabic.

== Grammar ==
=== Possessive prefixes ===
Rudes (1981) notes that Nottoway has two series of pronominal prefixes used for [[Inalienable possession|inalienable]] and alienable possession. Inalienable nouns, such as body parts, are possessed with the prefix ''ge''- 'my': '''''ge'''-snunke'' 'my hand', '''''ge'''-tunke'' 'my belly'. Alienable nouns are possessed with the prefix ''ak''- 'my': '''''ak'''-uhor'' '(my) old man', '''''aqu'''-eianha'' '(my) boy'. These two series of pronominal prefixes are also used on verbs, where they indicate the agent and patient, respectively. === Word order ===

Most of the written Nottoway materials are vocabularies rather than texts, so scholars can only make limited assumptions about the syntactic structure of the language. However, Rudes (1981) explains three syntactic characteristics that are supported by recorded Nottoway evidence:

1. The definite article precedes a noun, as in Tuscarora.
{{interlinear|indent=3
|'''Ha'''henũ
|'the thunder' (cf. Tuscarora '''''haʔ''' híʔnə̃ʔ'')}}

2. Of two adjacent nouns, the first noun modifies the second.
{{interlinear|indent=3
|Acquia ohonag
|deer skin
|'deer skin'}}

3. An adjective follows the noun it modifies, and most likely could also precede it.
{{interlinear|indent=3
|Unksawa wokenhu
|{} year
|'the new year'}}

Rudes tentatively reconstructs noun incorporation based on these examples:

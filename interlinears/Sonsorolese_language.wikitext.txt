The '''Sonsorolese language''' is a [[Micronesian languages|Micronesian language]] spoken in [[Palau]], originally on the islands composing the state of [[Sonsorol]], and spreading through migration elsewhere in the country. It is very close to [[Tobian language|Tobian]].

==Introduction==

===History===
Sonsorolese is mostly spoken in the [[Palau]] archipelago, particularly in Sonsorol, [[Pulo Anna|Pulo Ana]], and the Merir Islands.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.ethnologue.com/18/language/sov/|title = Redirected|date = 19 November 2019}}</ref> It is one of two indigenous languages spoken in the area.

===Population===
There are about 360 speakers spread out across 60 islands. Most speakers of Sonsorol are bilingual, with their second language being English.<ref name="web.archive.org">{{cite web |url=http://www.sonsorol.com/language/index_eng.htm |title=Archived copy |website=www.sonsorol.com |access-date=15 January 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070314051132/http://www.sonsorol.com/language/index_eng.htm |archive-date=14 March 2007 |url-status=dead}}</ref> The language is an official language for the areas where it is spoken. It is usually used for the state's internal communications, like announcements and invitations.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=http://www.sonsorol.com/language/|title=Language – Sonsorol island|language=en-GB|access-date=2020-01-24}}</ref> Some closely related languages of Sonsorol are [[Ulithian]], [[Woleaian]], and [[Satawalese]]. The language is part of the [[Austronesian language]] family. Most of the population have migrated from the islands of the Sonsorol state to Palau's main town, Koror and Echang village.<ref name=":0" /> The reasons are various, including economic and environmental. Young Sonsorolese speakers use a mixture of Palauan, English and Sonsorolese, what is called Echangese and is different from what the elder generation speaks.<ref name=":0" /> There are currently less than 20 speakers over 60 years old.<ref name=":0" />

==Phonology==

===Consonants===
In Sonsorolese, there are 19 [[consonants]]. These consonants are: /p/, /b/, /t/, /d/, /c/, /k/, /g/, /m/, /n/, /f/, /v/, /j/, /x/, /ɣ/, /r/, /w/, /s/, /ŋ/, and /ʟʲ/ (Capell, 1969).

===Vowels===
Sonsorolese has 5 vowels: /a/, /e/, /i/, /o/, and /u/. There are also diphthongs, including /ae/, /ai/, /ao/, and /au/. An example of the diphthong /ae/ is {{lang|sov|mae}}, which means "breadfruit" (Capell, 1969).

===Voiceless vowels===
[[Voicelessness#Voiceless vowels and other sonorants|Voiceless vowels]] occur in three contexts: “as finals, after a consonant, after a full, generally long vowel, and before a consonant, when they are acoustically similar to falling diphthongs, after non-final consonants a furtive /i/ or /u/ produces palatalization or velarisation (respectively) of the consonants” (Capell, 1969). {{clarify|date=March 2019}}

==Orthography and pronunciation==
Sonsorolese is primarily a [[spoken language]]. Many of the sounds are like those in [[Tobian language|Tobian]] and [[Woleaian language|Woleaian]]. A couple of [[dialect]]s include the pronunciation of ''d'', which is common at the beginning of words and similar to {{IPA|[ð]}}; ''r'' is pronounced as in Spanish; also, ''l'' is always pronounced with tongue touching the back roof of the mouth and sounds something like a combination of the {{IPA|[ɡ]}} and {{IPA|[l]}} sounds. For that reason, some Sonsorolese prefer to spell their els as {{angle bracket|ɡl}}. As in Woleaian, voiceless vowels are usually found at the end of Sonsorolese words. For example, in ''Dongosaro'', the native name for [[Sonsorol]] island, the final ''-o'' is voiceless.<ref name=":0" />

Written documents in Sonsorolese include the Constitution of Sonsorol State and certain parts of the Bible.<ref name=":0" /> However, there seems to be a confusion regarding the Bible since there seems not to be a distinction between Tobian and Sonsorolese. {{Citation needed|date=January 2020}}

==Grammar==

===Basic word order===
Sonsorolese’s basic [[word order]] is subject, object, verb (SOV).

An example of the use of the SOV word order is:
{{interlinear|indent=3|lang=sov
|Etai maho tipel
|I happy not
|subject object adverb
|I am not happy.}}

In questions, the word order stays the same.

*{{lang|sov|Emaho tipomu}} = Are you happy?

===Reduplication===
There is full [[reduplication]] in the Sonsorol language. E.g. 'orange' = {{lang|sov|hulu}}, 'oranges' = {{lang|sov|huluhulu}}.

===Numerals===
The numeral system of Sonsorolese is base-10. The [[numeral system]] can go up to 1,000, which is “da ngaladi”.<ref name="web.archive.org"/>

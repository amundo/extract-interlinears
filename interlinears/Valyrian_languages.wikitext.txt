The '''Valyrian languages''' are a fictional [[language family]] in the ''[[A Song of Ice and Fire]]'' series of fantasy novels by [[George R. R. Martin]], and in their television adaptation ''[[Game of Thrones]]''.

In the novels, '''High Valyrian''' and its descendant languages are often mentioned but not developed beyond a few words. For the TV series, language creator [[David J. Peterson]] created the High Valyrian language, as well as the derivative languages Astapori and Meereenese Valyrian, based on fragments from the novels.<ref name="Dothraki.com 31 March 2013">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |title=Valar Dohaeris |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/03/valar-dohaeris/ |website=Dothraki.com |date=31 March 2013 |access-date=27 April 2013 }}</ref> Valyrian and [[Dothraki language|Dothraki]] have been described as "the most convincing fictional tongues since [[Elvish languages (Middle-earth)|Elvish]]".<ref>{{cite news |title=The complex linguistic universe of "Game of Thrones" |url=https://www.economist.com/news/books-and-arts/21725752-dothraki-and-valyrian-are-most-convincing-fictional-tongues-elvish |date=5 August 2017 |access-date=4 August 2017 |work=The Economist}}</ref>

In the world of ''A Song of Ice and Fire'', High Valyrian occupies a cultural niche similar to that of [[Classical Latin]] in [[medieval Europe]].<ref name="TIME 3 May 2013">{{cite news |last=Tharoor |first=Ishaan |title=Tongues of Ice and Fire: Creating the Languages in ''Game of Thrones'' |url=http://entertainment.time.com/2013/05/03/tongues-of-ice-and-fire-creating-the-languages-of-game-of-thrones/ |newspaper=[[Time (magazine)|Time]] |date=3 May 2013 |access-date=3 May 2013 }}</ref>  The novels describe it as no longer being used as a language of everyday communication, but rather as a language of learning and education among the nobility of Essos and Westeros, with much literature and song composed in Valyrian.

===Creation===
[[File:David j peterson 2019 06 03.jpeg|thumb|[[David J. Peterson]], creator of the spoken Valyrian languages for ''[[Game of Thrones]]'']]
To create the [[Dothraki language|Dothraki]] and Valyrian languages to be spoken in ''[[Game of Thrones]]'', [[HBO]] selected the language creator [[David J. Peterson]] through a competition among [[constructed language|conlangers]]. The producers gave Peterson a largely free hand in developing the languages, as, according to Peterson, George R. R. Martin himself was not very interested in the linguistic aspect of his works.<ref name="TIME 3 May 2013" /> The already published novels include only a few words of High Valyrian, including ''valar morghulis'' ("all men must die"), ''valar dohaeris'' ("all men must serve") and ''dracarys'' ("dragonfire"). For the forthcoming novel ''[[The Winds of Winter]]'', Peterson has supplied Martin with additional Valyrian translations.<ref name="TIME 3 May 2013" />

Peterson commented that he considered Martin's choice of ''dracarys'' unfortunate because of its (presumably intended) similarity to the Latin word for dragon, ''{{lang|la|draco}}''. Because the [[Latin Language|Latin language]] does not exist in the fictional world of ''A Song of Ice and Fire'', Peterson chose to treat the similarity as coincidental and made ''dracarys'' an independent [[lexeme]];<ref name="Peterson 22 April 2013 comment-1257">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/sesir-urnebion-z%c8%b3hon-keliton-issa/#comment-1257 |title=Sesīr Urnēbion Zȳhon Keliton Issa (comment at 10:12 pm) |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=22 April 2013 |access-date=27 April 2013 }}</ref> his High Valyrian term for dragon is ''zaldrīzes''. The phrases ''valar morghulis'' and ''valar dohaeris'', on the other hand, became the foundation of the language's [[grammatical conjugation|conjugation]] system.<ref name="TIME 3 May 2013" /> Another word, ''trēsy'', meaning "son", was coined in honour of Peterson's 3000th Twitter follower.<ref name="Declension party">{{cite web |last=Peterson | first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/05/t%C8%B3ni-tresi/ |title=Tȳni Trēsi |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=20 May 2013 |access-date=25 May 2013 }}</ref>

Peterson did not create a High Valyrian [[writing system]] at the time, but he commented that he "was thinking something more like Egyptian's system of [[hieroglyph]]s—not in style, necessarily, but in their functionality. Egyptians had an alphabet, of sorts, a couple of phonetically-based systems, and a logography all layered on top of one another."<ref>{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |title=Tīkuni Zōbrī, Udra Zōbriar (comment on 10 April 2013 at 11:53 pm) |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/tikuni-zobri-udra-zobriar/#comment-1086 |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=10 April 2013 |access-date=27 April 2013 }}</ref> In the third season's episode "[[The Bear and the Maiden Fair]]", Talisa is seen writing a Valyrian letter in the Latin alphabet, because according to Peterson, "it didn't seem worthwhile to create an entire writing system for what ultimately is kind of a throwaway shot".<ref name="Peterson 13 May 2013">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/05/gryves-se-rina-litse/ |title=Gryves se Riña Litse |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=13 May 2013 |access-date=22 May 2013 }}</ref>

At the start of June 2013, there were 667 High Valyrian words.<ref>{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |title=Kastāmiro Daomior (comment at 2:46 am) |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/06/kastamiro-daomior/#comment-1854 |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=4 June 2013 |access-date=25 June 2013 }}</ref>

Vowels with a macron over them (ī, ȳ, ū, ē, ō and ā) are long, held for twice as long as short vowels. Some words are distinguished simply by their vowel length in High Valyrian. The rounded vowels {{angle bracket|ȳ}} and {{angle bracket|y}} may not be pronounced in modern High Valyrian, as a non-native or [[prestige (sociolinguistics)|prestige]] language and did not survive into the descendant languages. As a result, while [[Daenerys Targaryen]]'s first name may generally be pronounced {{IPA|[də.ˈnɛː.ɹɪs]}} by characters in ''[[Game of Thrones]]'', in High Valyrian it would have been closer to {{IPA|[ˈdae.ne.ɾys]}}, with a [[diphthong]] in the first syllable and a [[close front rounded vowel|rounded vowel]] in the last. The long vowels have also been lost in some derived languages; in [[Game of Thrones (season 3)|season 3]] of ''Game of Thrones'', we hear Astapori Valyrian, from which all long vowels have been lost.<ref name="Dothraki.com 8 April 2013"/>

Syllable stress is penultimate unless the penultimate syllable is [[syllable weight|light]] and the antepenultimate syllable is heavy, in which case stress is on the antepenultimate.<ref>{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |title=Tīkuni Zōbrī, Udra Zōbriar (comment at 4:47 pm) |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/tikuni-zobri-udra-zobriar/#comment-1075 |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=10 April 2013 |access-date=27 April 2013 }}</ref> As a highly [[inflection|inflected]] language, [[word order]] is flexible (a feature lost in derived languages),<ref name="Dothraki.com 8 April 2013"/> but sentences with relative clauses are [[head-directionality parameter|head-final]].<ref name="Dothraki.com 22 April 2013"/>

Nouns have eight cases—[[nominative case|nominative]], [[accusative case|accusative]], [[genitive case|genitive]], [[dative case|dative]], [[locative case|locative]], [[instrumental case|instrumental]], [[comitative case|comitative]] and [[vocative case|vocative]], though the instrumental and comitative are not distinguished in all declensions,<ref name="Case and number">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/sesir-urnebion-z%c8%b3hon-keliton-issa/#comment-1256 |title=Sesīr Urnēbion Zȳhon Keliton Issa (comment at 5:19 pm) |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=23 April 2013 |access-date=27 April 2013 }}</ref> nor are the genitive, dative and locative always distinguished in the plural.<ref name="Declension party"/> Both [[preposition]]s and [[postposition]]s are used to form other cases; for example, the ablative is formed with the preposition ''hen'' + the locative (e.g. ''hen lentot'', "from a house") while the superessive is formed with the postposition ''bē'' following the genitive (e.g. ''lento bē'', "on top of a house").<ref name="artoflanguageinvention133">{{cite book|last=Peterson|first=David J.|title=The Art of Language Invention|page=133}}</ref>

There are four [[grammatical gender]]s, which do not align with biological sex.<ref>{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/sesir-urnebion-z%c8%b3hon-keliton-issa/#comment-1279 |title=Sesīr Urnēbion Zȳhon Keliton Issa (comment at 10:24 am) |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=24 April 2013 |access-date=27 April 2013 }}</ref> The Valyrian names for the genders are:<ref name="Noun classes"/>

: ''hūrenkon qogror''—"lunar class",
: ''vēzenkon qogror''—"solar class",
: ''tegōñor qogror''—"terrestrial class",
: ''embōñor qogror''—"aquatic class".

[[Animacy|Animate]] and [[individuation|individuatable]] nouns are generally in the lunar or solar classes, while other nouns are generally classified as terrestrial or aquatic. The names of the classes derive from the nouns themselves, which are prototypical members of each gender.<ref name="Noun classes">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/perzo-vujita/#comment-1391 |title=Perzo Vūjita (comment at 12:30 am) |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=1 May 2013 |access-date=3 May 2013 }}</ref> Peterson describes Valyrian gender as being inherent but more predictable from [[phonology]] than [[french grammar#Gender|gender in French]], with some of the [[derivation (linguistics)|derivational]] properties of the [[noun class]]es of [[Bantu languages]].<ref name="Noun classes"/> As a result of the phonological predictability, many words for humans (which tend to end ''-a'' or ''-ys'') are lunar or solar; many foods and plants (often ending ''-on'') are terrestrial.<ref>{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/perzo-vujita/#comment-1418 |title=Perzo Vūjita (comment at 2:50 pm) |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=1 May 2013 |access-date=3 May 2013 }}</ref>

According to Peterson, "what defines declension classes in High Valyrian" can be divined by paying "close attention to the singular and plural numbers" and noting "where cases are conflated and where they aren't".<ref name="Conjugation party">{{cite web |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/05/some-high-valyrian-inflection/ |title=Some High Valyrian inflection |author-link=David J. ====Verbs====
Nouns have four grammatical numbers, but verb conjugations have only been described in the singular and the plural;<ref name="Conjugation party"/> paucals trigger plural agreement and collectives trigger singular agreement.<ref name="comment 2124">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/06/some-more-high-valyrian-inflection/#comment-2124 |title=Some More High Valyrian Inflection (comment at 12:45 pm) |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=28 June 2013 |access-date=30 June 2013 }}</ref> There are two paradigms of verbs, those with a stem ending with a consonant and those with a stem ending with a vowel;<ref name="Conjugation party"/> the tables below show examples of four tenses in the [[active voice]]. It is possible to tell which paradigm is in use from the [[grammatical person|first person]] [[plural]] [[indicative]]—consonant stems will always end in ''-i'', whereas vowels stems will end in ''-ī''.<ref name="Conjugation party"/> Verbs with stems ending in a vowel follow a pattern where that stem-terminal vowel might change—''-a'' and ''-i'' do not change, ''-e'' becomes ''-i'', but ''-o'' and ''-u'' both become ''-v''.<ref name="Conjugation party"/> Verb stems may never end in a long vowel or a diphthong.<ref name="Conjugation party"/> There is a subjunctive mood that is not only used in subordinate clauses, but also in all negative statements.<ref name="artoflanguageinvention142">{{cite book|title=The Art of Language Invention|first=David J.|last=Peterson|page=142}}</ref>

====Adjectives====
Adjectives have three declension classes.<ref name="Adjectives">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/07/valyrian-adjectives/ |title=Valyrian Adjectives |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=1 July 2013 |access-date=6 July 2013 }}</ref> Like verbs, adjectives only have two number forms—a singular, which is also used for the collective, and a plural, also used for paucal numbers.<ref name="Adjectives"/> Adjectives may be both prepositive (e.g. "the ''white'' shoe") or [[post-positive adjective|post-positive]] (e.g. "the body ''politic''"); when prepositive some further rules apply.<ref name="Adjectives"/>

Several forms of [[elision]] and [[assimilation (linguistics)|consonant assimilation]] occur with prepositive adjectives:

* With inflections of two syllables (such as ''kast'''oti''''' in several class I plurals), the second syllable is often lost to elision; word-final ''-t'' is also lost before consonants—compare ''ader'''ot''' ābrot'' ("to the quick woman") with ''ader'''o''' Dovaogēdot'' ("to the quick Unsullied").<ref name="Adjectives"/>
* When such elision causes a word-final ''-z'' (such as with the class I ''kasta'' becoming ''kastyzy'' ([[nominative case|nom.]]) and ''kastyzys'' ([[vocative case|voc.]]) in the lunar plurals, below), the final ''-z'' is [[consonant voicing and devoicing|devoiced]] to ''-s'' when it precedes a voiceless consonant—compare ''kast'''ys''' hobresse'' ("blue goats") with ''kast'''yz''' dāryssy'' ("blue kings"), both forms from ''kastyzy'', the lunar nominative plural.<ref name="Adjectives"/>
* If the syllable in question is vowel–consonant–vowel, then only the final vowel is elided—compare ''ānogro ēl'''ȳro''''' ("of the first blood") to ''ēl'''ȳr''' ānogro'' ("of the first blood").<ref name="Adjectives"/>
* Whereas [[instrumental case|instrumental]] forms are generally listed as containing ''-s-'' or ''-ss-'' and [[comitative case|comitative]] forms generally contain ''-m-'' or ''-mm-'', some nouns use only the ''s''-forms in both cases and some nouns use the ''m''-forms for both. When this occurs, the consonant in question experiences [[consonant harmony]], causing the use of what might otherwise be a comitative form for an instrumental and vice versa, {{citation needed span|such as with the examples given of class I vowel mutations below,|date=July 2013}} where the forms appear to be instrumental ("by means of the ... men", "by means of the ... rains"), despite being comitative ("accompanying the ... man", "accompanying the ... rains").<ref name="Adjectives"/>
* Finally, word-final ''-m'' is decreasingly common in High Valyrian. Contracted inflections that end in ''-m'' will often assimilate to ''-n'' unless the next word begins with a vowel or a [[labial consonant]].<ref name="Adjectives"/>

=====Class II and III adjectives=====
Adjective classes II and III both conflate rather more forms, failing to distinguish between solar and lunar nouns and failing to distinguish between terrestrial and aquatic nouns.<ref name="Adjectives"/> Class II also has some subclasses, that have not yet been detailed.<ref name="Adjectives"/> The exemplars used here are the class II adjective ''adere'' ("sleek, smooth, slippery, fast, quick") and the class III adjective ''ēlie'' ("first").<ref name="Adjectives"/>

Class III adjectives also experience vowel changes when subject to the elision described above. When a lunar or solar form is elided to a syllable containing ''-ȳ-'' (such forms are highlighted in the table below), this ''-ȳ-'' mutates to ''-io-''; this does not occur with terrestrial or aquatic forms. Compare:<ref name="Adjectives"/>

: ''valosa ēl'''ȳse'''''—"with the first man" (''vala'' is a lunar noun of the first declension)<ref name="Declension party"/>
: ''ēl'''ios''' valosa''—"with the first man"
: ''daomȳssi ēl'''ȳssi'''''—"with the first rains" {{citation needed span|(''daomio'' is a lunar noun of the third declension)|date=July 2013}}
: ''ēl'''ȳs''' daomȳssi''—"with the first rains"

=== Duolingo course ===

On October 31, 2016 a course in High Valyrian for English speakers began to be constructed in the [[Duolingo]] Language Incubator. David J. Peterson is one of the contributors to the course.<ref name="duolingo-wikia">{{cite web|url=http://duolingo.wikia.com/wiki/High_Valyrian_for_English|title=High Valyrian for English|work=Duolingo Wiki|access-date=4 April 2017}}</ref><ref name="duolingo-incubator">{{cite web|url=https://incubator.duolingo.com/courses/hv/en/status|title=Course Status: High Valyrian for English Speakers|work=Duolingo Language Incubator|access-date=5 April 2017}}</ref> The beta version was released on July 12, 2017.<ref name="duolingo-wikia"/><ref name="duolingo-incubator"/> In April 2019, the course was updated in anticipation of [[Game of Thrones]]' eighth and final season. As a part of this update, Peterson created audio for the course's lessons and exercises.

== Bastard Valyrian ==
In the world of the novel and TV series, the [[World of A Song of Ice and Fire#Free Cities and vicinity|Nine Free Cities of Essos]] speak locally evolved variants of Valyrian known as '''Bastard Valyrian''', described by the character [[List of A Song of Ice and Fire characters#Tyrion Lannister|Tyrion]] in ''[[A Dance with Dragons]]'' as "not so much a [[dialect]] as nine dialects on the way to becoming separate tongues".<ref>''A Dance with Dragons'', Tyrion I.</ref>

Peterson described the relationship between High Valyrian and Bastard Valyrian as being similar to that between [[Classical Latin]] and the [[Romance languages]] evolved from [[Vulgar Latin]], or more accurately between [[Classical Arabic]] and the [[varieties of Arabic|modern varieties of Arabic]], in that High Valyrian is intelligible, with some difficulty, to a speaker of a local Essoan language.<ref name="Dothraki.com 22 April 2013" />

=== Slaver's Bay dialects ===
The slave cities of [[World of A Song of Ice and Fire#Slaver's Bay|Slaver's Bay]] are lands of the ancient Ghiscari Empire that was conquered and annexed by Valyria, so they speak related languages descended from High Valyrian with the substrate of the local Ghiscari languages.<ref>[http://www.dothraki.com/2014/05/the-state-of-valyrian/ The State of Valyrian]</ref>  Peterson noted that with regard to the vocabulary of the derived languages, "If it’s got a 'j' in an odd place, it’s probably Ghiscari in origin."<ref>[http://www.dothraki.com/2014/05/the-valyrian-word-for-hamster/ The Valyrian Word for Hamster]</ref>

==== Astapori Valyrian ====
{{Quote box
|quote  = ''Si kizy vasko v’uvar ez zya gundja yn hilas.'' <p>"And this because I like the curve of her ass."
|source = — Astapori Valyrian, ''[[Game of Thrones]]'', season 3, [[Walk of Punishment|episode 3]]<ref name="Vulture 23 April 2013">{{cite news |last=Martin |first=Denise |title=Learn to speak Dothraki and Valyrian from the man who invented them for ''Game of Thrones'' |url=http://www.vulture.com/2013/04/game-of-thrones-dothraki-language-inventor.html |access-date=24 April 2013 |newspaper=Vulture |date=23 April 2013 }}</ref>
|width  = 33%
}}
The first derivative Valyrian language to be featured in the series was Astapori Valyrian, a variety from the city of [[World of A Song of Ice and Fire#Astapor|Astapor]] in Slaver's Bay. It appeared in the third-season premiere episode "[[Valar Dohaeris]]".  Peterson created the Astapori dialogue by first writing the text in High Valyrian, then applying a series of regular grammar and sound changes to simulate the changes in natural languages over a long period of time.<ref name="CNN What's Next">{{Cite web |first=David |last=Peterson |author-link=David J. Peterson |url=http://whatsnext.blogs.cnn.com/2013/03/29/david-peterson-and-the-languages-of-game-of-thrones/ |title=David Peterson and the languages of ''Game of Thrones'' |work=[[CNN]] What's Next |access-date=4 April 2013 }}</ref>

For example, Astapori Valyrian has lost all long vowels (designated with a [[Macron (diacritic)|macron]]) and most [[diphthong]]s.<ref name="Dothraki.com 8 April 2013"/> Between vowels, [b, d, g] have become [v, ð, ɣ]; subsequently, [p, t, k] have become [b, d, g] in the same environment.<ref name="artlanguage168169">{{cite book|last=Peterson|first=David J.|title=The Art of Language Invention|page=168-169}}</ref>
As a result, an "Unsullied" is rendered as ''Dovaogēdy'' {{IPA|[do.vao.ˈɡeː.dy]}} in High Valyrian, but as ''Dovoghedhy'' {{IPA|[do.vo.ˈɣe.ði]}} in Astapori.<ref name="Dothraki.com 8 April 2013"/> Similarly, Astapori Valyrian has lost the [[grammatical case|case system]] of High Valyrian, so the [[word order]] is more reliably [[subject–verb–object]] (SVO) and the four genders of High Valyrian have been reduced to two, with three [[definite article]]s: ''ji'' {{IPA|[ˈʒi]}} (animate singular, derived from High Valyrian ''ziry'' {{IPA|[ˈzi.ry]}} "him/her (accusative)"), ''vi'' {{IPA|[ˈvi]}} (inanimate singular, derived from High Valyrian ''ūī'' {{IPA|[ˈuː.iː]}} "it (accusative)"), and ''po'' {{IPA|[ˈpo]}} (plural, derived from High Valyrian ''pōnte'' {{IPA|[ˈpoːn.te]}} "them (accusative)").<ref name="Dothraki.com 8 April 2013"/><ref name="artlanguage196">{{cite book|last=Peterson|first=David J.|title=The Art of Language Invention|page=196}}</ref> There is also an indefinite article, ''me'' {{IPA|[ˈme]}}, derived from High Valyrian ''mēre'' {{IPA|[ˈmeː.re]}} ("one").<ref name="artlanguage196"/> [[Stress (linguistics)|Word stress]] is less predictable than in High Valyrian, but commands are stressed word-finally (for example: ''ivetrá'').<ref name="Dothraki.com 15 April 2013">{{cite web |last=Peterson |first=David J. |author-link=David J. Peterson |title=Qilōnario Geron |url=http://www.dothraki.com/2013/04/qilonario-geron/ |publisher=Dothraki.com |date=15 April 2013 |access-date=27 April 2013 }}</ref>

{{expand section|date=May 2017}}
Meereenese Valyrian appears in Seasons 4 and 6 of ''Game of Thrones''.<ref name="season4-meereenese">[https://wiki.dothraki.org/Season_4_Meereenese_Valyrian_Dialogue Season 4 Meereenese Valyrian Dialogue]</ref> Like Astapori Valyrian, it lacked long vowels as well as the sound /y/.<ref>[https://wiki.dothraki.org/Meereenese_Valyrian_Phonology Meereenese Valyrian Phonology]</ref> However, its phonology departs considerably more from High Valyrian; for example, "Unsullied" is ''Thowoá'' [θo.woˈa], whereas in High Valyrian it is ''Dovaogēdy'' [do.vao.ˈɡeː.dy] and in Astapori Valyrian it is ''Dovoghedhy'' [do.vo.ˈɣe.ði].<ref name="season4-meereenese"/><ref name="meereenese-phonology">[http://wiki.dothraki.org/Meereenese_Valyrian_Phonology Meereenese Valyrian Phonology]</ref>

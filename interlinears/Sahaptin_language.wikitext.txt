'''Sahaptin''' or '''Shahaptin''', endonym '''Ichishkin''',<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Leonard|first1=Wesley Y.|last2=Haynes|first2=Erin|date=December 2010|title=Making "collaboration" collaborative: An examination of perspectives that frame linguistic field research|url=http://scholarspace.manoa.hawaii.edu/handle/10125/4482|journal=Language Documentation & Conservation|volume=4|pages=269–293|issn=1934-5275}}</ref> is one of the two-language [[Sahaptian languages|Sahaptian]] branch of the [[Plateau Penutian languages|Plateau Penutian]] family spoken in a section of the northwestern plateau along the [[Columbia River]] and its tributaries in southern Washington, northern Oregon, and southwestern Idaho, in the [[United States]];<ref>[[#refMithun1999|Mithun, 1999]]</ref> the other language is [[Nez Perce language|Nez Perce]] or ''Niimi'ipuutímt''. Many of the tribes that surrounded the land were skilled with horses and trading with one another; some tribes were known for their [[horse breeding]] which resulted in today's [[Appaloosa]] or [[Cayuse horse]].{{relevance inline|date=October 2021}}

The word ''Sahaptin/Shahaptin'' is not the one used by the tribes that speak it, but from the Columbia Salish name, '''Sħáptənəxw / S-háptinoxw''', which means "stranger in the land". This is the name the [[Wenatchi]] (in Sahaptin: Winátshapam) and Kawaxchinláma (who speak Columbia Salish) traditionally call the [[Nez Perce people]]. Early white explorers mistakenly applied the name to all the various Sahaptin speaking people, as well as to the Nez Perce. Sahaptin is spoken by various tribes of the Washington Reservations; Yakama, Warm Springs, Umatilla; and also spoken in many smaller communities such as [[Celilo Village, Oregon|Celilo]], Oregon.

The [[Yakama]] tribal cultural resources program has been promoting the use of the traditional name of the language, ''Ichishkíin Sɨ́nwit'' (″this language″), instead of the Salish term ''Sahaptin''.<ref>Beavert, Virginia and Hargus, Sharon ''Ichishkíin sɨ́nwit yakama = Yakima Sahaptin dictionary''. Toppenish, Wash.: Heritage University, Seattle: in association with the University of Washington Press, 2009; 492 pp. OCLC 268797329.</ref>

==Grammar==
There are published grammars,<ref>[[#refJacobs1931|Jacobs, 1931]].</ref><ref>[[#refRigsby and Rude1996|Rigsby and Rude, 1996]].</ref> a recent dictionary,<ref>[[#refBeavert & Hargus2009|Beavert & Hargus, 2009]].</ref> and a corpus of published texts.<ref>[[#refJacobs1929|Jacobs, 1929]].</ref><ref>[[#refJacobs1937|Jacobs, 1937]].</ref>

Sahaptin has a [[split ergativity|split ergative]] syntax, with direct-inverse voicing and several applicative constructions.<ref>[[#refRude2009|Rude, 2009]].</ref>

The [[ergative case]] inflects third-person nominals only when the direct object is first- or second-person (the examples below are from the Umatilla dialect):

{| class=wikitable
| {{interlinear
|i- q̓ínu -šana yáka '''paanáy'''
|3.NOM- see -ASP bear '''3SG.ACC'''
|'the bear saw him'}}
| {{interlinear
|i- q̓ínu -šana '''{{=}}aš''' yáka '''-nɨm'''
|3.NOM- see -ASP '''{{=}}1SG''' bear '''-ERG'''
|'the bear saw me'}}
|}

The direct-inverse contrast can be elicited with examples such as the following. In the inverse, the transitive direct object is coreferential with the subject in the preceding clause.

{| class=wikitable
!Direct
!Inverse
|-
| {{interlinear
|wínš i- q̓ínu -šana wapaanłá -an ku '''i-''' ʔíƛ̓iyawi -ya '''paanáy''' 
|man 3.NOM- see -ASP grizzly -ACC and '''3.NOM-''' kill -PST '''3SG.ACC'''
|'the man saw the grizzly and he killed it'}}
| {{interlinear
|wínš i- q̓ínu -šana wapaanłá -an ku '''pá-''' ʔiƛ̓iyawi -ya
|man 3.NOM- see -ASP grizzly -ACC and '''INV-''' kill -PST
|'the man saw the grizzly and it killed him'}}
|}

The inverse (marked by the verbal prefix ''pá-'') retains its transitive status, and a patient nominal is case marked accusative.

{{interlinear|indent=3
|ku '''pá-''' ʔiƛ̓iyawi -ya wínš -na
|and '''INV-''' kill -PST man -ACC
|'and it killed the man' ({{=}} 'and the man was killed by it')}}

A semantic inverse is also marked by the same verbal prefix ''pá-''.

{| class=wikitable
!Direct
!Inverse
|-
| {{interlinear
|q̓ínu -šana '''{{=}}maš'''
|see -ASP '''{{=}}1SG/2SG'''
|'I saw you'}}
| {{interlinear
|'''pá-''' q̓inu -šana '''{{=}}nam'''
|'''INV-''' see -ASP '''{{=}}2SG'''
|'you saw me'}}
|}

In Speech Act Participant (SAP) and third-person transitive involvement, direction marking is as follows:

{| class=wikitable
!Direct
!Inverse
|-
| {{interlinear
|'''á-''' q̓inu -šana {{=}}aš '''paanáy'''
|'''OBV-''' see -ASP {{=}}1SG '''3SG.ACC'''
|'I saw him/her/it'}}
| {{interlinear
|'''i-''' q̓ínu -šana {{=}}aš '''pɨ́nɨm'''
|'''3.NOM-''' see -ASP {{=}}1SG '''3.ERG'''
|'he/she/it saw me'}}
|}
